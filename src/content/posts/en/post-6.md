Having been away from the workplace for more than a year, I've realized that making real changes truly requires an extended break, and this period has its own profound significance.

### 1. Relaxing the Body and Mind

Putting down everything, important or otherwise, slowing down, and returning to a state of calm are essential for an objective understanding of life.

### 2. Forgetting the Past

Only an extremely slow passage of time can wash away the behaviors and mindsets from past work and life.

These changes demand a deeply pure state of mind to occur. Just as in Jin Yong's novels, to learn Tai Chi, one must first forget. Only by forgetting can one truly learn; only an empty cup can hold water.

### 3. Cultivating Metacognition

After emptying everything out and starting anew, the first task is to correct the fundamental concepts and perceptions of things.

By reflecting with the guidance of some good books, I've questioned the perceptions I've built around work and life, and identified those that are incorrect. For instance, common beliefs like 'work can make you rich,' 'you must be diligent to succeed,' or 'learning means memorizing answers for exams' are all profoundly wrong. In reality, work often doesn't lead to great wealth, and has a relatively low leverage ratio. True financial freedom is freedom of time and choice. What matters more than diligence is doing the right thing. Learning is primarily about cultivating thinking and analytical skills, not memorizing standard answers.

Therefore, the unexamined common sense that people follow might be wrong. The Truman Show is a fictional film, yet it exists in our daily lives. The crucial question is how to build your own trustworthy reliance in an untrustworthy world.

Only by continuously improving one's metacognition can one find a relatively reliable fulcrum, upon which to build a more dependable and broader set of capabilities.

For example, I always found technical and professional education and work to be somewhat repulsive and dull. Now, with greater understanding, I can explain why: they are incomplete. They only teach professional knowledge, but neglect to teach technical culture, value metrics, and, more importantly, learning and analytical abilities.

Why are teaching and work designed this way? It might be due to simply limited teaching quality, but more likely, it’s because they are inherently adulterated by the processes of selection, industrial needs, and the demand for obedience. Frankly, they are not designed for your personal development, but serve some grander agenda—they need to mass-produce industrial workers.

The book 《Hackers: Heroes of the Computer Revolution》 has been immensely beneficial. It not only chronicles the history of computers from research to home use but also vividly documents the people behind the computer revolution—the formation of their abilities, their values, their subculture, and how these were diluted and later returned as the field developed. I also read Linus Torvalds' autobiography, and you realize that technology is not a step-by-step curriculum, a standard-answer exam, nor a cold document, and definitely not a matter of all-night overtime. Technology is vibrant and real. It is driven by people who are full of curiosity and courage for the world. They love life, love technology, focus on what is interesting, useful, and possess taste and strong individuality. Only when you acquire these qualities and become this type of person can you truly understand and engage in technology itself.

Therefore, for personal development, one must recognize the flaws within these frameworks, spot the bugs in the 'tampered world,' and 'hack' everything.

### 4. Rebuilding the Personal Operating System

Based on more accurate metacognition and associations of things, I am rebuilding my personal understanding, moral compass, values, and capabilities.

Then, based on these new personal capabilities, I will rebuild my social life, personal life, technical skills, and investment strategies.

Only by undergoing a complete transformation can one achieve true elevation.

I've observed a common trait among successful people in both technology and investment: they all possess strong metacognitive abilities. They are certainly not focused on diligence or hard work, nor are they merely greedy for wealth. They are more concerned with understanding and hacking the entire system, hoping to gain a certain return and achievement through their understanding. That is, they use their metacognitive ability to deeply understand the system, then continuously iterate and verify their understanding, thus forming a positive feedback loop and a deeper comprehension of the world.

### 5. Doing The Right Things

Doing the right things is significantly more important than merely doing things correctly. I've seen this concept in the books of Li Xiaolai and 'Left-Ear Rabbit' (Zou Xin), as well as in the actions of people like Steve Jobs and Steve Wozniak. This applies well to both investing and technology.

Doing the right things is paramount, even if it means moving a little slower. What currently might not look like the right path, might, in fact, be the only true path.
